# Understanding Your DSRCT Journey

A guide for patients, families, and caregivers


**Every patient's path through DSRCT is their own.** Some people move through all three phases of this journey. Others spend years in Phase 1 or reach remission and stay there. This guide is not a roadmap with a fixed destination. It is a way of understanding where you are, what to focus on now, and what may lie ahead, so that nothing comes as a complete surprise and you are never without context.

## *Phase 1* Diagnosis, Induction Therapy &amp; Surgery

**Your priority in Phase 1:** A DSRCT diagnosis is a long marathon, and your challenge is to approach it like an elite athlete. Stay as strong and healthy as possible despite the rigorous treatments your body will endure. Keeping up nutrition, managing side effects, and staying on schedule all matter. So does your quality of life right now. Supportive care is not a reward for finishing treatment; it is part of treatment. The intensity of this experience can lead to burnout, and being aware of that risk from the start, and actively investing in your comfort, your spirit, and the reasons you are fighting, can help you go the distance. A diagnosis of DSRCT presents many unknowns and few guarantees. Focus on what you can have agency of each day.

1. **Diagnosis confirmed with the FISH test (Fluorescence in situ Hybridization)**
    The EWSR1-WT1 gene fusion is the hallmark genetic marker of DSRCT, detected through molecular pathology testing and a key part of confirming the diagnosis.
2. **Induction chemotherapy: VDC/IE (the Ewing's Protocol, borrowed from Ewing sarcoma treatment due to the shared biology of fusion-driven pediatric sarcomas)**
    This multi-drug chemotherapy protocol is designed to shrink the tumor before surgery and reduce the overall burden of disease in the body. It is a *systemic* treatment, meaning it travels through the bloodstream to reach cancer cells throughout the entire body, not just in one location. This is important because DSRCT often involves widespread disease at diagnosis, and systemic chemotherapy addresses what imaging may not fully capture.

The drugs in this regimen:
  VDC alternates with IE in cycles, typically every 2 to 3 weeks. The number of cycles varies by patient based on response, tolerance, tumor size, and the timing of surgical intervention.
**VDC cycles:** Vincristine &middot; Doxorubicin (Adriamycin) &middot; Cyclophosphamide
**IE cycles:** Ifosfamide &middot; Etoposide

**Common side effects:** Each drug contributes its own profile. Not everyone experiences all of them, and your team will monitor you closely and provide supportive medications throughout.


| Side Effect | What to Know   |
| --- | --- |
| Nausea &amp; vomiting | Very common; anti-nausea medications are routinely given |
| Fatigue | Often significant; rest and nutrition support are important |
| Low blood counts | Raises infection risk; may require transfusions or growth factor injections |
| Hair loss | Typically temporary |
| Mouth sores | Mucositis, especially associated with doxorubicin |
| Infection risk | Fever during treatment requires immediate contact with your care team |
| Kidney &amp; bladder effects | Ifosfamide requires aggressive IV hydration to prevent hemorrhagic cystitis |
| Heart effects | Doxorubicin carries cumulative cardiac risk; cardiac monitoring is standard |
| Neuropathy | Tingling, numbness, or pain in hands and feet; associated with vincristine |
| Cognitive effects | Difficulty with memory and concentration during and after treatment, sometimes called chemo brain |


These side effects can be managed. Talk to your care team early and often about what you are experiencing. You do not need to push through symptoms silently. Effective supportive care during induction is part of getting to surgery.



- **Local control: surgery**

    Total surgical resection (complete removal) is the goal, but debulking, which means removing as much disease as possible even when complete removal is not yet achievable, can still be meaningful. Reducing tumor burden can buy valuable time, allow other treatments to work more effectively, and in some cases create a path toward further surgery down the road. HIPEC (heated chemotherapy delivered directly into the abdomen during surgery) may also be an option.

    DSRCT treatment for most patients relies on a multimodal approach, combining systemic chemotherapy, targeted radiation, and surgery together to achieve remission. What cannot be fully removed surgically may be controllable through radiation or chemotherapy, and what is not removable today may become removable after further treatment response.

- **Alternative local control: radiation therapy**

    When surgery is not possible, due to tumor location, extent of disease, or patient condition, radiation therapy becomes an important tool for local control. Targeted radiation can shrink tumors, slow progression, and in some cases reduce disease burden enough to make future surgery possible. It may also be used to treat specific sites of disease that cannot be safely resected. Even when cure is not the immediate goal, radiation can provide meaningful disease control and improve quality of life. It should always be discussed as part of the full multimodal treatment picture with your care team.



### Build Your Care Team from the Start

Finding Your SUPER ONC Team

- DSRCT-experienced surgeon (better outcomes are linked to surgeons who have performed this procedure before)
- DSRCT disease expert consultation for guidance, or second opinion
- Palliative care for symptom management and recovery support
- Integrative oncology to optimize a patient's physical, mental, and emotional well-being, manage treatment side effects, and improve overall quality of life
- Oncology dietitian or nutritionist to support your nutrition
- Physical and occupational therapy (PT and OT) to mitigate treatment side effects, and support maintaining strength and activity levels


## *Phase 2* NED &amp; Maintenance Therapy

**Your goal in Phase 2:** Rebuild your strength, stay vigilant, and live well. DSRCT carries a significant risk of recurrence even after achieving NED, which is why ongoing therapies, regular monitoring, and maintaining your overall health all remain important. Keeping your body strong and your spirit resilient is not just about quality of life; it may also help you tolerate future treatment if it is ever needed.

<div class="callout-label">What NED means</div>
**NED = No Evidence of Disease.** This means clean surgical margins and clear scans. It is an important milestone and a real reason for hope.

<div class="callout-label">Scanxiety is real, and you are not alone in it</div>
Anxiety in the days and weeks surrounding scans is one of the most commonly reported experiences in this journey. The fear of what a scan might show is completely understandable, and it does not go away just because things have been stable. Acknowledging it rather than pushing through it is important. A few things that can help: talking openly with your care team about how you are feeling before and after scans, having a plan for the waiting period that keeps you engaged and connected, leaning on your support community, and working with a counselor familiar with cancer-related anxiety. You do not have to white-knuckle your way through scan days.


- **WART: whole abdominal radiation therapy**
Even after a successful surgery, microscopic cancer cells too small to see with the naked eye or detect on imaging can remain in the abdomen. WART delivers radiation across the entire abdominal area to target and destroy those cells before they can grow. It is one of the most important tools for reducing the risk of recurrence after surgery and is sometimes given at the same time as maintenance chemotherapy.

- **Maintenance chemotherapy**
Common options include Irinotecan plus Temozolomide (Temodar), or Cyclophosphamide and Topotecan. Your team will recommend the best fit for you.

- **Regular monitoring**
Bloodwork and imaging scans every 3 to 6 months. Being on a clinical trial often means even more frequent monitoring, which can be an added benefit.

- **Review ongoing chemotherapy after extended NED**
After a long period of NED, your team may discuss whether continuing systemic chemotherapy is still the right choice for you, carefully weighing the risks against the benefits.


### Your Whole Self Needs to Heal Too

Reaching NED is meaningful, but coming out of survival mode brings its own challenges. Grief, anxiety, and uncertainty are common. Phase 2 is the time to shift some focus from fighting cancer to nurturing your health in the fullest sense: mind, body, and spirit. Counseling, spiritual care, and positive experiences are not extras. They are part of healing, and part of building a life that feels worth living.

Consider exploring approaches that support both body and spirit:

- Counseling or therapy to process your experience
- Spiritual care or faith community support
- Prioritizing movement, sunshine, and time in nature
- Meaningful experiences and time with people you love
- Finding connection and community with those who can understand
- Creative expression through art, writing, or music to process and tell your story
- Using your experience to be of service to others


## *Phase 3* Recurrent or Refractory Disease

**Your goal in Phase 3:** Keep living with intention and keep fighting smart. Phase 3 is not the end of the road. It is the reality for the majority of people who go through this experience, and many navigate it for years. Some patients achieve NED a second or even third time. Others keep the disease controlled and stable for extended periods. The path forward may look different than Phase 1, but there is still hope on this path, and the goal is to walk it as fully as possible.


- **Surgery or radiation for local control** Additional surgeries or targeted radiation may be used to attempt to re-establish disease control.
- **Clinical trials** Targeted immunotherapies and novel agents based on molecular pathology. Clinical trials may offer access to treatments not yet widely available.
- **Off-label or repurposed drugs** Some medications approved for other conditions may help manage symptoms or slow disease progression. Discuss with your oncologist.
- **Palliative care** Palliative care focuses on comfort, symptom relief, and quality of life at any stage of illness, not just end of life. You can receive it alongside active treatment.
- **Hospice care** When treatment is no longer the focus, hospice teams provide compassionate end-of-life support for both patients and families.
- **Honoring your wishes at end of life** You have the right to advocate for how you want to experience the end of your life. Advance directives, conversations with loved ones, and your care team can help ensure your wishes are respected.

### A note on palliative care
Palliative care is not giving up. It is **expert medical support designed to help you feel better and live better,** and it works best when it starts early, alongside any other treatment you are receiving.

### The Ripple Effect: Caring for the Whole Circle

A DSRCT diagnosis does not only affect the person who has it. The fear, grief, and trauma radiate outward to partners, parents, children, siblings, close friends, and entire communities. The weight on a caregiver quietly carrying their own anguish while trying to stay strong, or a parent watching their child suffer, is real and profound. These are not secondary concerns.

Recognizing the size of that impact and making space for everyone in the circle to receive support is not a distraction from care. It is part of it. No one should be navigating this alone, and no one's pain in this experience is too small to tend to.

Support for the whole circle may include:

- Individual counseling for patients and caregivers
- Family therapy or guided family conversations
- Peer support groups for patients and loved ones separately
- Grief support, including the grief that can come before a loss occurs
- Spiritual or pastoral care for patients and family members
- Community connection through the DSRCT patient and family network
