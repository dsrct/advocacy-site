A guide for patients, families, and caregivers

## Overview
Desmoplastic small round cell tumor (DSRCT) is an extremely rare and aggressive cancer. It is
classified as a sarcoma, meaning it arises from connective tissue rather than organs. In most cases,
DSRCT begins on the lining of the abdominal & pelvic cavity, though for many at diagnosis it may have
spread to nearby organs and lymph nodes.

DSRCT most often affects adolescents and young adults, and is significantly more common in males
than females. Because it tends to grow silently in the abdomen, it is frequently diagnosed at an
advanced stage. Often pain, deformity, or fatigue is reported as a first symptom, but can present in
unexpected ways, contributing to delay in diagnosis.

DSRCT was first identified and described in medical literature in 1989 by pathologists Gerald and
Rosai. It is one of the youngest named cancers in oncology, which contributes to the lack of overall
research and disease information available.

## The Biology of DSRCT
Every case of DSRCT is defined by a specific chromosomal change: a fusion of two genes called
EWSR1 and WT1. This is called the EWSR1-WT1 gene fusion, and it occurs on chromosomes 11 and
22.

This gene fusion acts like a faulty switch that stays stuck in the "on" position. It signals the body to
overproduce proteins that drive tumor growth and help tumors build their own blood supply. Identifying
this specific gene fusion is what separates DSRCT from other similar-looking cancers under a
microscope.

The name of the disease reflects what it looks like in pathology: "desmoplastic" refers to the dense,
fibrous tissue surrounding the tumor cells; "small round cell" describes the appearance of the cancer
cells themselves.

## Why DSRCT Is Classified as a Pediatric Cancer

DSRCT is considered a pediatric cancer, even though most patients are teenagers, young adults, or
sometimes older adults. This can be confusing. The classification has nothing to do with the age of the
patient. It has everything to do with the biology of the tumor.

### Cancer type is determined by biology, not by who gets it

Adult cancers typically develop through decades of accumulated damage: repeated exposures, genetic
mutations building up over time, environmental wear on cells. Pediatric cancers work differently. They
tend to arise from errors in early developmental processes, often involving a single gene fusion event
that hijacks the normal programs cells use during fetal development.

DSRCT fits the pediatric pattern in every meaningful way. It is driven almost entirely by one event, the
EWSR1-WT1 gene fusion, rather than by the kind of accumulated mutations seen in lung cancer, colon
cancer, or other adult malignancies. Gene expression studies show that DSRCT cells look more like
mesenchymal progenitor cells active during fetal development than like any adult tissue. The tumor
typically carries very little genomic disruption beyond the fusion itself.

In other words, even when DSRCT appears in a 35-year-old, the cancer itself is behaving like
something that went wrong in early development, not something caused by decades of adult life.

### Other cancers follow the same pattern

DSRCT is not unusual in this respect. Several cancers are classified as pediatric even though they
regularly appear in adults, including Ewing sarcoma, rhabdomyosarcoma, and medulloblastoma. These
tumors keep the pediatric classification because the molecular identity of the tumor determines the
category, not the patient's age at diagnosis.

DSRCT's median age at diagnosis is around 22 years, but the range extends from childhood into the
50s and beyond. That range does not change what the tumor is.

### Why this matters for treatment

The pediatric classification is not just a label. It shapes which clinical trials DSRCT patients may be
eligible for, which research consortia study the disease, and which therapeutic approaches are most
likely to be relevant. Because DSRCT has a low mutation burden, it tends not to respond well to
immunotherapy approaches designed for adult cancers with high mutation loads. Research is instead
focused on targeting the fusion protein itself, transcriptional pathways, and chromatin remodeling, all
approaches developed from the broader field of pediatric cancer biology.

## How DSRCT Is Diagnosed

Diagnosis requires a biopsy of tumor tissue, which is then analyzed by a pathologist. Because
DSRCT's cancer cells can look similar to several other tumors under a standard microscope, confirming
the diagnosis requires molecular testing.

The standard molecular test used is called Fluorescence in situ Hybridization (FISH). FISH detects
the EWSR1-WT1 gene fusion directly in the tumor sample and is considered essential for an accurate
DSRCT diagnosis. Often this can cause a delay, if samples have to be sent to specialized facilities/labs
for testing.

### Why the FISH test matters for your diagnosis

Several other cancers can look nearly identical to DSRCT in a standard biopsy sample, which is why a
diagnosis based on appearance alone is not considered reliable. These include Ewing sarcoma,
rhabdomyosarcoma, neuroblastoma, Wilms tumor, lymphoma, and small cell carcinoma. The
EWSR1-WT1 fusion detected by FISH is what separates DSRCT from tumors that may look the same
but require very different treatment.

If you or a family member received a diagnosis at a center unfamiliar with rare sarcomas, or if a
diagnosis was made without molecular testing, requesting a second opinion from a high-volume
sarcoma center is strongly encouraged. Accurate pathologic diagnosis is the foundation of appropriate
treatment.

## How Common Is DSRCT?

If you have been told there are only about 200 reported cases of DSRCT, that number comes from a
specific source and has important context.

The figure originates from a 2014 review of the SEER database (a national cancer registry maintained
by the National Cancer Institute) covering cases reported between 1973 and 2007. That review
identified 192 cases. A 2022 SEER analysis covering 1975 to 2018 found 154 cases over a longer
period.

### Why these numbers likely undercount true incidence

FISH technology was not widely implemented in sarcoma diagnosis until approximately 2010 to 2012.
These DSRCT registry figures were collected during a period when the primary diagnostic tool, FISH
testing, was only just being adopted in pathology departments. Cases diagnosed before widespread
FISH use may have been misclassified and recorded under a different diagnosis. Cases seen at
centers without access to molecular testing may not have been correctly identified at all.

In addition, SEER captures only a portion of U.S. cancer cases and does not include cases that were
never correctly diagnosed. The true number of people who have had DSRCT is unknown and likely
higher than published registry figures suggest.

The most current medical literature reports DSRCT incidence at approximately 2 in every 10 million
people per year, a figure that has remained consistent across multiple studies, and one that still reflects
the same limitations in detection and reporting described above.

## Treatment

There are no standardized treatment guidelines for DSRCT. Because so few cases are diagnosed each
year, large clinical trials are extremely difficult to conduct. Current treatment is guided by expert
consensus and the published experience of high-volume sarcoma centers.

Research published in 2022 found that patients who received surgery combined with both
chemotherapy and radiation had better survival outcomes than those who received surgery and
chemotherapy alone. This supports the multimodal approach used at leading treatment centers.

Note that medications, doses and timing can vary from patient to patient. There is no “one plan fits all”
in DSRCT treatment.

Most treatment protocols combine:
- Chemotherapy: High-dose chemotherapy to reduce tumor burden, using multiple chemo
agents given in combination.
- Surgery: Cytoreductive surgery to remove as much visible tumor as possible (called
‘debulking’), is sometimes combined with heated chemotherapy delivered directly into the
abdominal cavity (HIPEC, or hyperthermic intraperitoneal chemotherapy)
- Radiation: Radiation therapy, whole abdominal radiation to treat any molecular cells remaining
after complete resection surgery, or targeted radiation on inoperable disease sites


Emerging and evolving treatment approaches being studied include:
- Immunotherapy: Immunotherapy for DSRCT is not yet a standard treatment, but it is one of the
most active areas of current research. Approaches being studied include targeted antibody
therapies, mRNA vaccine trials, and CAR T-cell therapy. Clinical trials are ongoing, and this is
one of the strongest reasons families are encouraged to connect with a high-volume sarcoma
center that treats DSRCT.
- Precision Oncology: Precision oncology looks at the specific biology of a tumor to find targets
that may be unique to that cancer. In DSRCT, researchers have identified proteins found on the
surface of tumor cells that are rarely present on healthy tissue, making them potential targets for
treatment, though most of these approaches are still being evaluated in early-phase clinical
trials. For many families, precision oncology becomes essential at disease recurrence, after
standard therapies have been exhausted, or when systemic chemotherapy cannot be tolerated.
Connecting with a precision oncologist who has experience in rare sarcomas can open doors to
trials and treatment options.
- Integrative Oncology: Integrative oncology supports the whole person during treatment
through mind-body practices, sleep, nutrition, and physical activity. These approaches do not
treat DSRCT directly but play an important role in managing side effects and maintaining
strength. Most major cancer centers have integrative oncology clinicians on staff who can help
families navigate these choices safely alongside active treatment.

## Prognosis

DSRCT is an aggressive disease. Despite treatment, many patients experience recurrence, and most
published survival data reflects outcomes of fewer than three years from diagnosis.

These numbers are hard to sit with, and we do not want to minimize them. They also reflect outcomes
from a time when far less was understood about this disease and fewer treatment options existed.
Research into targeted therapies, immunotherapies, and precision oncology is opening new directions
that were not available even a few years ago.

Long-term disease-free survivors exist, and their numbers are growing as treatment has improved.
Survivors at five years and beyond have been documented, though “long term survivorship” definitions
vary across studies, and many patients far outlive the expectations of their diagnosing doctors.
There are many versions of the DSRCT experience, and every patient’s journey is unique. No statistic
can predict the path or outcome for any individual patient.

## You Are Not Alone in This

A DSRCT diagnosis is rare enough that most oncologists will never see a case in their career. Talking
to someone who has been through it is different from talking to someone who has only read about it.

The DSRCT Hope Always Foundation hosts two ongoing virtual peer support groups for patients,
caregivers, and those who have lost someone to this disease:
- An active-case peer support group for patients and families currently navigating diagnosis or
treatment
- A grief support group for those who have lost a loved one to DSRCT
Both groups are facilitated with lived experience and a commitment to honest, grounded support. You
are welcome to bring the hard questions, to find connections among those who truly understand, and to
share experiences or just listen. Wherever you are at in your DSRCT journey, you are welcome.
